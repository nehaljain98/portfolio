CSTEM Center

July - August 2019

C-STEM (Computing, Science, Technology, Engineering, & Mathematics) is a UC Approved Educational Prep Program for Undergraduate Admission for both K-12 & Community College students at all UC campuses.

During the summer of my 3rd year of college, I joined the UC Davis External Tools team as a part-time contract Web Production Designer.

Context

Overview

The UC Davis CStem website is one of the most visited university websites. The site has a lot of different users, but is especially relevant to the 30,000+ K-12 student population that use the site at some point during their robotic and coding career as it contains all of the information about the program's requirements. Both students and faculty are frustrated with the poor web experience and have difficulty finding information. For this reason, I collaborated closely with the team to define, develop, and deliver visual designs in a consumer-facing environment.The ask was to create a web page to inform and educate users about all the opportunities and events offered by the center with the use of UC Davis design systems.

My Role

As one of the co-designers, I was responsible for designing the UX, rendering layouts, and leading visual refinements. I created frameworks and prototypes to share the vision, design principles, and content strategy. Also, I contributed to user research, interviews and surveys. I helped to conceptualize ideas and drive decision making.

Timeline

Over the eight week timeline, we had four creative reviews where the project manager gave feedback and critiques, and one stakeholder review where multiple stakeholders gave higher-level business direction. I roughly had three weeks of total design time, as I had 18 days for the initial design development and then 1-2 days between reviews for edits.

Project Brief 

The old design showcases bad user experience which has been a source of frustration for both UC Davis students and faculty. Therefore, the university administrators asked us to explore solutions to the older site's problems.

We needed to find a way to conduct user research and use UC Davis style guides to inform our redesign. We wanted to validate our work using a final prototype, which we would present to a group of university stakeholders making it easier to explain and justify our design choices.

⚡️ The Challenge: Balancing visual design with an efficient user experience

The CSTEM Center website must aesthetically and strategically use existing web components and UC Davis design guides while having a consistent experience across all the web pages.

User Research

Surveys

We chose to use a survey to discover what the different user segments are. We deliberated on whether an online or an in-person survey was more appropriate. We picked the most effecient user reseach method given our limited resources and decided on online survey.

We posted the survey on various faculty and student groups on Facebook. We also emailed school administrators with the survey. In total, we got 56 responses from students and 26 from administrators. Through the survey we gained an understanding of who was using the site, and what they were using it for.

Users and Personas

To get more understanding of what these users’ goals and pain points on the site were, we conducted more in-depth interviews where we would walk through the website with 5 people from each user group. During the interviews, we performed a contextual inquity (or walkthrough) of the website with our interviewees to observe how they used the site and what information they wanted to access.
We also asked them both pre-established and follow-up questions about their goals and habits as users.

Revised Sitemap

We restructured the site to facilitate the access of information from the use cases (the information that they desired most).

1. Since students are the majority of the user population, we made sure that all of the information that they need is within the “Curriculum” section because that’s where they tend to look first (based off of our interview observations). 4. The news and events were also cleanly categorized under one nav tab which allows for a smoother user experience.

Wireframing

We created layouts based on user goals and UCD web patterns. After inserting and removing pages, and rearranging pages on the sitemap, we were able to start sketching new website layouts. As we sketched and wireframed, we explored layouts that followed existing UC Davis website patterns, but also entertained new ideas that could help the CSTEM website establish its own unique character.

During the wireframing process, we decided that using more modern UC Davis website design patterns would be the best layout choice for several reasons.

Result

We designed a set of new features within the CSTEM website that supports a much wider range of needs. The new design takes new and returning users on a more controlled, personalized journey and uses unique features through the navigation bar that guides users smoothly to their destination. By integrating these features and using the UC Davis branding, the new CSTEM website creates a more full, guided experience for users and builds a more lasting relationship with them.

Takeaways

Executing Design-to-Development Handoff

This project gave me valuable experience in handing off deliverables for development in a consumer-facing environment. I made a PDF guide with development notes detailing the component types and usage, ensured pixel perfection within organized layers in my final AdobeXD file, and exported all assets with correct naming conventions. This guide helped the developers quickly localize the page for the global market and launch the page as fast as possible.

Implementation

After the usability tests, Valeria and I used a Powerpoint and Functional Specification Report to present our process and results to key stakeholders in the CSTEM department. They liked our ideas, however, they also explained to us that the department was facing internal debate. Since, the department was facing some budget issues, they could not spare funds for the development of the websites. The intent was to use our research and redesigns to keep for future purposes when they will be able to allocate capital for the online experience.

We understood why there would be hesitation in regards to spending financial capital as well as time on this endeavour:

1. Changing the site is a lot of work and will require a team of developers
2. Changing the site will force people who are used to using the site to relearn the new site

This gave us a glimpse into the real world where research efforts may not always be financially viable. It was a little bit disappointing to see progress hindered by such matters, but I am still grateful to have gotten the opportunity to learn from this experience. While, it hasn't been implemented yet, there is hope for the future where this research and prototype will be useful..

Thinking Forward!

1. Get user feedback while wireframing rather than making personal choices about the layout.
2. Try to increase the number of participants in the interviews and usability tests to get more accurate results.
3. Understand the hidden variables and obstacles between the project and its implementation by asking more questions to clients.
