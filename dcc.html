The Story
My team and I were challenged to design the website to best serve the UC Davis Design Community. For the purpose of all the stakeholders, we planned and executed market research, user research, user testing, prototypes, visual design, and collaborated directly with the product manager and other stakeholders. The entire design department depends on the club to contact alumni and recruiters for the development of design students.

Our objective was to create a multi-purpose website that could continue for the course of the club. Since the club's leadership changes on an annual basis, we decided to use a website building platform such as Weebly for the sake of longevity. We discussed coding the website from scratch and inviting developers to the team but this would have worked against the club and the objective of a continued legacy for the website.

My Role:

As the President of the Design Careers Club, I designed the website with a talented team over the course of a quarter. Under the supervision of Professor Young, we created wireframes, addressed and considered user profiles, and implemented the best course of action for the club.
Problem Space
Why does the organization need a website?
The organization needs a website as it is a bridge between students and alumni. We also cater to the design department faculty and design recruiters. The organization is an essential part of the department because it organizes annual events and quarterly workshops that are attended by industry professionals to give access to a diverse range of career opportunities.
Since, the organization is also funded by the department, it is important to have a professional online presence that gives an accurate representation of the club's goals and its impact.
Space for opportunity
What if we had a one-stop shop for all our user's needs?
The club had no website which made it harder for us to reach our users about all the various things we were working on. Because of this, a lot of the students didn't know all the things that club was offering. Being scattered was hurting the organisation because the faculty also wanted easier access to all the opportunities and events. For that purpose, we wanted to create a cohesive website with all different opportunities offered. Wanting to offer multiple variety of things expanded our user base.
The Solution
The solution was to create a responsive website which was not coded such that it could continue with the succession of club leadership. We decided to base our designs on user research and user analytics so that we can satisfy user needs and wants. Moreover, we decided to do usability testing to understand shortcomings so that the organisation can continue to work on the design and make it more efficient.

Needs & Personas

Who are our users? Since the user types are straight forward, we categorized them into 5 user groups:

Design Students: This website will be used by the students to navigate design workshops, design events and job opportunities. They can also refer to the blog which will have professional articles such as preparing for the events.

Design Faculty: The design faculty will have a similar goal of being aware of the events. They will also need to be able to get information on alumni and events from the previous years.

UC Davis Design Alumni: The alumni will be connected to current design students through this website and will initially begin their mentorship program throught the website

Recruiters: The recruiters will be encouraged to connect and employ current design students that they meet at club events

The DCC Board: This website will need to be accessed and edited by the board members. They will maintain relevant information which requires editing to be easy and the site to work for longevity.

The following are the core problems this design aims to solve:

Information Architecture
Organizing and grouping user flows for effecient navigation.
Since the organization of information on the website was an issue brought up in our personas, we wanted to take a look at the persona use cases and see:
1. Where their final page destinations were on the sitemap
2. What user navigation paths looked
3. The person icons indicate the ending page for each of the personas' navigation paths.

We wanted to make sure that all of our personas, especially the international students, could access their information quickly without having to read a lot of text. So we restructured the site to facilitate the access of information from the use cases, (the information that they desired most).
Since students are the majority of the user population, we made sure that all of the information that they need is within the ‚ÄúAcademics‚Äù section because that‚Äôs where they tend to look first (based off of our interview observations). To do this, we regrouped and added some relevant pages to the section. Previously, academic information was scattered outside of ‚ÄúAcademics‚Äù in sections like ‚ÄúAbout‚Äù and ‚ÄúPrograms & Services‚Äù. Now, more relevant information is in the section, and as seen in the revised sitemap, more students‚Äô use cases fall in ‚ÄúAcademics‚Äù.
We also wanted to include multiple paths for users to reach their desired page. This way, no matter what the user navigation decisions, users can reach their destinations more quickly.
Wireframes
We wanted to connect the site's information architecture to its visual design by showing paths between pages. Wireframing aided us in clarifying consistent ways for displaying particular types of information on the user interface. As you can see from the sketches, we explored intended functionality in the interface and worked on different landing pages. Our main focus was on the home page because there isn't a lot of information so we wanted to gain and keep attention for the users to be able to reach the destination they wanted.

Reflection
Looking Forward
In the end, the succession of leadership is responsible for the continuation of the website. We took on the responsibility of creating the website based on user research. We still feel that the organisation should do a lot more research into design systems because there was an overwhelming number of synonymous design patterns for club organisations. Moreover, we would have preferred to code the website as we had the skills but unfortunately we decided to go with Weebly so that it is easy to edit for the future board members. We think that the website will benefit from being coded and hosted as the interaction and animation will improve the website. However, it is important to understand the limits and parameters of a project and we respected the criterion.
